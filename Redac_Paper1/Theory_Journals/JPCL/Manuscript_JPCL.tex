\title{Quantum-coherent spectral engineering: non-Markovian dynamics in photosynthetic energy transfer under engineered photon baths}

\begin{abstract}
Spectral engineering of the photon bath incident on the Fenna--Matthews--Olson (FMO) complex extends excitonic coherence lifetimes by \SIrange{20}{50}{\percent} and enhances excitonic energy transfer efficiency by \SI{25}{\percent} at \SI{295}{\kelvin}. Using the numerically exact Process Tensor Hierarchy of Pure States method with Spectrally Bundled Dissipators (PT-HOPS/SBD), we show that dual-band transmission windows centered at \SIlist{750;820}{\nano\meter} selectively populate vibronic resonances while suppressing population of overdamped bath modes that dominate pure dephasing. In the polaron frame, this selective excitation reduces the effective system-bath coupling, extending the $1/e$ coherence decay time from $\tau_c = \SI{280(25)}{\femto\second}$ to \SI{420(35)}{\femto\second}. The enhancement is robust against static disorder ($\sigma = \SI{50}{\per\centi\meter}$), with an ensemble-averaged relative enhancement $\eta = 0.20 \pm 0.04$ over \num{500} disorder realizations. These findings establish spectral bath engineering as a design principle for coherence-assisted transport, with testable predictions for two-dimensional electronic spectroscopy under filtered illumination.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Quantum coherence in biological light-harvesting complexes has attracted sustained attention since two-dimensional electronic spectroscopy (2DES) revealed long-lived oscillatory signals in the Fenna--Matthews--Olson (FMO) complex of green sulfur bacteria.\cite{Engel2007,Panitchayangkoon2010} Although the origin of these oscillations---whether electronic, vibronic, or vibrational---remains under debate,\cite{Cao2020,Christensson2012} excitonic coherences persist at \SI{295}{\kelvin} for timescales ($\gtrsim \SI{300}{\femto\second}$) comparable to energy transfer rates, suggesting that system-bath correlations play a constructive role.\cite{Ishizaki2009a}

Several theoretical mechanisms have been proposed to explain coherence-assisted transport: environment-assisted quantum transport (ENAQT),\cite{Mohseni2008,Rebentrost2009} vibronic enhancement,\cite{Chin2013,Huelga2013} and non-Markovian memory effects arising from structured spectral densities.\cite{Tanimura1989,Suess2014} Crucially, all of these studies treat the spectral environment as a \textit{fixed} property of the protein-solvent bath. A fundamentally different question is whether \textit{external control of the incident photon spectrum} can modulate the effective system-bath coupling and thereby extend quantum coherence.

Here, we introduce \textbf{spectral bath engineering}: deliberate spectral shaping of the incident radiation field to selectively populate vibronic resonances that promote coherence-assisted energy transfer. This approach differs from classical spectral optimization (which maximizes absorbed photon flux) in that it targets the quantum-mechanical resonance condition between the filter transmission profile and the vibronic coupling structure of the chromophore network.

We employ the seven-bacteriochlorophyll-\textit{a} (BChl~\textit{a}) FMO monomer\cite{Adolphs2006} as a benchmark system, using numerically exact PT-HOPS/SBD simulations\cite{Citty2024a} that capture non-Markovian environmental memory without invoking secular, Markov, or weak-coupling approximations. The method has been benchmarked against Hierarchical Equations of Motion (HEOM)\cite{Tanimura1989,Ishizaki2009a} and the TEMPO tensor network approach,\cite{Strathearn2018} establishing its validity for structured spectral densities.

Our central result is that dual-band spectral filtering at \SIlist{750;820}{\nano\meter} extends excitonic coherence lifetimes by \SIrange{20}{50}{\percent} and enhances the energy transfer efficiency by \SI{25}{\percent}, through a mechanism traceable to selective population of vibronically resonant channels and reduced pure dephasing in the polaron frame.

\section{Theory and methods}

\subsection{Open quantum system framework}

The reduced density matrix $\vb*{\rho}(t)$ evolves according to:
\begin{equation}
\pdv{\vb*{\rho}(t)}{t} = -\frac{i}{\hbar}\comm{\hat{H}_S}{\vb*{\rho}(t)} + \mathcal{D}[\vb*{\rho}(t)],
\label{eq:master_eq}
\end{equation}
where $\hat{H}_S$ is the system Hamiltonian and $\mathcal{D}$ represents the dissipator capturing system-bath interactions. The electronic Hamiltonian reads:
\begin{equation}
\hat{H}_{\mathrm{el}} = \sum_{n=1}^{7} \varepsilon_n \dyad{n} + \sum_{n \neq m} J_{nm} \dyad{n}{m},
\label{eq:excitonic_hamiltonian}
\end{equation}
with site energies $\varepsilon_n$ and electronic couplings $J_{nm}$ from Ref.~\citenum{Adolphs2006}.

\subsection{Spectral density engineering}

Each chromophore couples to a local bath described by a composite spectral density:\cite{Moix2011}
\begin{equation}
J_{\mathrm{bath}}(\omega) = \frac{2\lambda\gamma\omega}{\omega^2 + \gamma^2} + \sum_k \frac{2\lambda_k\omega_k^2\gamma_k}{(\omega-\omega_k)^2 + \gamma_k^2},
\label{eq:spectral_density}
\end{equation}
where the Drude--Lorentz term describes overdamped protein-solvent fluctuations ($\lambda = \SI{35}{\per\centi\meter}$, $\gamma^{-1} = \SI{106}{\femto\second}$), and the Brownian oscillator terms represent underdamped vibronic modes at $\omega_k = \SIlist{150;200;575;1185}{\per\centi\meter}$.\cite{Chin2013}

We define the \textit{effective driving spectral density} via:
\begin{equation}
J_{\mathrm{eff}}(\omega) = T(\omega) \cdot J_{\mathrm{solar}}(\omega),
\label{eq:filtered_spectral_density}
\end{equation}
where $T(\omega)$ is the transmission function of the optical filter and $J_{\mathrm{solar}}(\omega)$ is the AM1.5G solar spectral irradiance. The key distinction is that $T(\omega)$ modifies the \textit{driving field} that prepares the initial excitonic state, not the protein-bath spectral density $J_{\mathrm{bath}}(\omega)$ itself. The transmission function is:
\begin{equation}
T(\omega) = T_{\mathrm{peak}} \sum_{i=1}^{2} w_i \exp\!\left[-\frac{(\omega - \omega_{c,i})^2}{2\sigma_i^2}\right],
\label{eq:transmission_function}
\end{equation}
where $\omega_{c,i}$ target vibronic resonances and $\sigma_i$ control the bandwidths.

\subsection{PT-HOPS/SBD method}

Simulations use the PT-HOPS method with SBD.\cite{Suess2014,Citty2024a} The stochastic hierarchy evolves as:
\begin{equation}
\pdv{t}\ket{\psi^{(\vb{n})}} = \left(-i\hat{H}_S - \sum_j n_j \nu_j\right)\ket{\psi^{(\vb{n})}}
- i\sum_j \sqrt{(n_j+1)d_j}\, \hat{L}_j \ket{\psi^{(\vb{n}+\vb{e}_j)}}
- i\sum_j \sqrt{n_j/d_j}\, \hat{L}_j^\dagger \ket{\psi^{(\vb{n}-\vb{e}_j)}},
\label{eq:hops}
\end{equation}
where $\vb{n}$ is the hierarchy multi-index encoding non-Markovian bath memory, $\nu_j$ are Padé-decomposed bath frequencies, $d_j$ are the corresponding expansion coefficients, and $\hat{L}_j$ are system-bath coupling operators.\cite{Duan2017} All simulations use hierarchy depth $L = 6$, $K = 3$ Padé terms at $T = \SI{295}{\kelvin}$, and are verified via a 12-test validation suite benchmarked against HEOM (\SI{1.8}{\percent} relative deviation; trace conservation $< \num{5e-13}$; see Supporting Information).

\section{Results and discussion}

\subsection{Coherence extension}

We quantify coherence using the $l_1$-norm,\cite{Baumgratz2014} a basis-dependent measure accessible through state tomography:
\begin{equation}
C_{l_1}(\rho) = \sum_{i \neq j} \abs{\rho_{ij}}.
\label{eq:l1_coherence}
\end{equation}
We define the coherence lifetime $\tau_c$ as the $1/e$ decay time of $C_{l_1}(t)$. Under optimal dual-band filtering, $\tau_c = \SI{420(35)}{\femto\second}$ at \SI{295}{\kelvin}, compared with $\tau_c = \SI{280(25)}{\femto\second}$ under broadband illumination---corresponding to a \SI{50}{\percent} extension at the upper bound of the confidence interval (Figure~\ref{fig:quantum_dynamics}a).

Shaded regions: \SI{95}{\percent} confidence intervals.}
\label{fig:quantum_dynamics}

The inverse participation ratio, $\mathrm{IPR} = (\sum_n \abs{\mel{n}{\hat{\rho}}{n}}^2)^{-1}$, increases from $\mathrm{IPR} \approx 4.1$ (broadband) to $\mathrm{IPR} \approx 6.8$ (filtered), indicating enhanced delocalization across the seven-site network. This delocalization increases the number of constructively interfering pathways available for energy transfer.

\subsection{Energy transfer enhancement}

We define the excitonic energy transfer efficiency (ETE) as:
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{ETE} = k_{\mathrm{trap}} \int_0^{t_{\max}} \Tr[\hat{P}_{\mathrm{RC}}\,\vb*{\rho}(t)]\,\dd{t},
\label{eq:ETE}
\end{equation}
where $\hat{P}_{\mathrm{RC}} = \dyad{3}$ projects onto the reaction center site and $k_{\mathrm{trap}} = \SI{1}{\per\pico\second}$ is the phenomenological trapping rate.\cite{Ishizaki2009a} Filtered illumination enhances ETE by \SI{25(3)}{\percent} relative to broadband excitation (Table~\ref{tab:quantum_metrics}).

The enhancement mechanism involves selective population of vibronically resonant states. The filtered driving field preferentially excites modes satisfying:
\begin{equation}
\Delta E_{nm} \approx \hbar\omega_{\mathrm{vib}} \pm J_{nm},
\label{eq:resonance_condition}
\end{equation}
where $\Delta E_{nm} = \varepsilon_n - \varepsilon_m$ is the inter-site energy gap, $\omega_{\mathrm{vib}}$ is the underdamped vibronic frequency, and $J_{nm}$ is the electronic coupling. For the dominant $\ket{1} \to \ket{3}$ pathway ($\Delta E \approx \SI{150}{\per\centi\meter}$), the $\omega_{\mathrm{vib}} = \SI{575}{\per\centi\meter}$ mode mediates vibronically assisted transfer under \SI{750}{\nano\meter} filtering.\cite{Chin2013}

\subsection{Quantum metrics summary}

Table~\ref{tab:quantum_metrics} summarizes the quantum metric enhancements. The \SI{89}{\percent} concurrence enhancement reflects strengthened inter-site entanglement, while the \SI{59}{\percent} increase in Quantum Fisher Information (QFI) witnesses quantum-enhanced sensitivity:\cite{Pezze2009}
\begin{equation}
F_Q[\rho, \hat{O}] = \Tr[\rho \hat{L}_{\hat{O}}^2],
\label{eq:QFI}
\end{equation}
where $\hat{L}_{\hat{O}}$ is the symmetric logarithmic derivative with respect to the generator $\hat{O}$.

\subsection{Environmental robustness}

We define the relative enhancement:
\begin{equation}
\eta = \frac{\mathrm{ETE}_{\mathrm{filtered}} - \mathrm{ETE}_{\mathrm{broadband}}}{\mathrm{ETE}_{\mathrm{broadband}}},
\label{eq:eta_def}
\end{equation}
which quantifies the fractional gain from spectral filtering. The enhancement persists across physiologically relevant conditions (Figure~\ref{fig:robustness}). Maximum enhancement occurs at \SIrange{285}{300}{\kelvin}, coinciding with the operating temperature of green sulfur bacteria. At \SI{295}{\kelvin}, $\eta = 0.25$; at \SI{310}{\kelvin}, $\eta$ decreases to $0.18$ but remains statistically significant ($p < 0.01$).

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{ETR_Under_Environmental_Effects.pdf}
\caption{\textbf{Environmental robustness.} \textbf{(a)}~Temperature dependence of $\eta(T)$, showing optimal enhancement at \SIrange{285}{300}{\kelvin}. \textbf{(b)}~Histogram of $\eta$ over \num{500} static disorder realizations ($\sigma = \SI{50}{\per\centi\meter}$): $\expval{\eta} = 0.20 \pm 0.04$. Error bars: \SI{95}{\percent} confidence intervals.}
\label{fig:robustness}
\end{figure}

Under static disorder ($\sigma = \SI{50}{\per\centi\meter}$), the ensemble-averaged enhancement is $\expval{\eta} = 0.20(4)$ with a coefficient of variation of \SI{20}{\percent}. The 12-test validation suite confirmed computational accuracy: \SI{1.8}{\percent} relative deviation from HEOM steady-state populations, trace conservation $< \num{5e-13}$, and density matrix eigenvalues bounded above $\num{-1e-14}$ (Supporting Information).

\subsection{Physical mechanism}

The enhancement originates from selective state preparation in the polaron frame.\cite{Jang2008} The reorganization energy is:
\begin{equation}
\lambda = \int_0^\infty \frac{J_{\mathrm{bath}}(\omega)}{\omega}\,\dd{\omega}.
\label{eq:reorg_full}
\end{equation}
Spectral filtering modifies the initial excitonic state by restricting the driving field to frequencies that preferentially populate sites whose energy gaps match specific vibronic resonances. In the polaron-transformed frame, these selectively prepared states experience a reduced \textit{effective} coupling to the overdamped bath continuum:
\begin{equation}
\lambda_{\mathrm{eff}} = \int_0^\infty T(\omega)\,\frac{J_{\mathrm{bath}}(\omega)}{\omega}\,\dd{\omega} < \lambda,
\label{eq:effective_reorganization}
\end{equation}
where $T(\omega) \leq 1$. Since the pure dephasing rate scales as $\Gamma_{\mathrm{pd}} \propto \lambda_{\mathrm{eff}} k_B T$,\cite{Jang2008} filtered excitation suppresses dephasing while preserving the vibronic channels that assist transport---the Franck--Condon overlaps with discrete modes remain intact because $T(\omega)$ is nonzero at the resonant frequencies $\omega_k$.

\subsection{Limitations}

Several approximations constrain the present analysis. We restrict the model to the single-exciton manifold, excluding multi-exciton states that may become relevant at high excitation densities. The bath is assumed site-independent (identical spectral density for all chromophores), whereas protein inhomogeneity introduces site-dependent coupling.\cite{Cao2020} The reaction center trapping is modeled phenomenologically via a fixed rate $k_{\mathrm{trap}}$, neglecting its internal electronic structure. Finally, while our predictions are consistent with known FMO dynamics, direct experimental validation under filtered illumination remains to be performed; we propose 2DES with spectrally filtered pump pulses as a concrete test, where the predicted \SI{50}{\percent} coherence extension should manifest as prolonged cross-peak oscillations in the $\ket{1}$--$\ket{3}$ region of the 2D spectrum.

\section{Conclusion}

Using numerically exact PT-HOPS/SBD simulations, we have demonstrated that spectral bath engineering---deliberate shaping of the incident photon spectrum---extends excitonic coherence lifetimes by \SIrange{20}{50}{\percent} and enhances energy transfer efficiency by \SI{25}{\percent} in the FMO complex at \SI{295}{\kelvin}. The mechanism, analyzed within the polaron frame, involves selective suppression of pure dephasing via reduced effective system-bath coupling while preserving vibronic resonance channels that assist transport.

The key findings are: (i)~dual-band filtering at \SIlist{750;820}{\nano\meter} targets specific vibronic resonance conditions; (ii)~$\tau_c$ increases from \SI{280(25)}{\femto\second} to \SI{420(35)}{\femto\second}; (iii)~the enhancement is robust across \num{500} disorder realizations ($\expval{\eta} = 0.20 \pm 0.04$); and (iv)~a 12-test validation suite confirms numerical accuracy against HEOM.

These results are testable via 2DES under spectrally filtered excitation. The spectral engineering principle is not specific to FMO: it applies to any open quantum system where vibronic resonances contribute to transport, including organic photovoltaic donor--acceptor complexes and semiconductor quantum dot arrays.\cite{Cao2020} More broadly, this work demonstrates that the \textit{spectral structure} of the driving field---not merely its intensity---constitutes an independent control parameter for quantum coherence in condensed-phase systems.
